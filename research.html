Research

My research lies at the intersection of online optimization, Bayesian learning, emergency medical services, and humanitarian logistics. I am particularly interested in decision-making under uncertainty for time-critical, high-stakes environments.

Dissertation

Title: Online Optimization of Ambulance Dispatching in Mass Casualty Incidents with Bayesian Updates

Summary: I develop offline and online models for casualty transportation, integrating rolling-horizon optimization, Bayesian estimation of victim types, and heuristic/metaheuristic methods (GRASP, VNS). The goal is to improve survival outcomes and system robustness under uncertain and evolving conditions.

Current Research Themes

  • Online ambulance dispatching: Rolling-horizon MILP models with adaptive Bayesian updates of demand and victim type proportions.
  • EMS system dynamics & simulation: Modeling survival dynamics, hospital capacity, and feedback effects in mass-casualty incidents.
  • Fairness in humanitarian logistics: Distributional equity metrics for relief allocation, integrating Gini-based and other fairness measures.
  • Hybrid optimization–simulation frameworks: Combining exact models, heuristics, and simulation for policy evaluation and decision support.

Selected Projects

Rolling-Horizon Ambulance Dispatch with Bayesian Updates

Development of a dynamic dispatch model that updates victim type proportions using Bayesian learning, solved via MILP and GRASP/VNS-based heuristics. The model is evaluated under varying forecast accuracy and incident scenarios.

System Dynamics Model for EMS in Disaster Settings

A system dynamics simulation integrating ambulance operations, hospital triage, survival curves, and learning-based estimation of victim composition, enabling scenario analysis and policy testing.

Online Relief Distribution with Blocked Edges

Conceptually related to online post-disaster distribution problems with uncertain road availability, extension toward casualty transport and hospital access under disruption.
